CONFIDENTIAL

TEAM: NAVIGATION (NAV)

Role: Flight Dynamics Officer

Your Mission: You are responsible for plotting the rocket's course to Mars. You must choose a route and calculate the total travel distance so the Engineering team knows how much fuel to buy.

1. Route Selection

Discuss with your team and circle one route:

Route Option Distance to Mars Maneuvers Needed Hazard
ROUTE A (Fast) 140 Million Miles + 10 Million Miles Asteroid Field! (Tell ENG to buy a Repair Bot)
ROUTE B (Safe) 140 Million Miles + 60 Million Miles None. (Safe travel, but uses more fuel)

2. Official NASA Math Calculation

Rockets don't fly in straight lines; they have to maneuver around planets. Calculate your total travel miles.

140 (Distance) + (Maneuvers) = Total Miles

📖 Space Glossary

  • Trajectory: The curved path a rocket takes through space. It relies on gravity to help "slingshot" the ship forward.
  • Asteroid Field: A region of space filled with floating rocks. Hitting one at high speed can puncture the hull.

🚀 Career Pathway: Flight Dynamics Officer (FDO)

"FIDO" controllers sit in Mission Control and use math to make sure the rocket ends up exactly where it needs to be. It's like throwing a dart at a moving target while you are also moving!

Middle School: Focus on Algebra, Geometry, and Physics.
College: Get a B.S. (Bachelor of Science) in Aerospace Engineering or Applied Mathematics.
NASA: Apply for the Pathways Internship program to train directly in Mission Control!
RESTRICTED

TEAM: OPERATIONS (OPS)

Role: Life Support Engineer

Your Mission: You must calculate the exact "Payload Mass" (weight) of the food, water, and oxygen needed to keep the astronauts alive. If you mess up the math, they run out of air!

1. Mission Parameters

  • Crew Size: 4 Astronauts
  • Base Food per Astronaut: 500 kg (Kilograms)

2. Official NASA Math Calculation

Calculate the total mass of the life support payload.

4 (Astronauts) x 500 (kg of food) = Total Mass (kg)

⚠️ TELL THE ENGINEER:

If your total mass is over 2,500 kg, tell them to buy "Advanced Life Support." If it's 2,500 kg or less, tell them to buy "Standard Life Support."

📖 Space Glossary

  • Payload: Anything a rocket carries that isn't fuel. This includes satellites, rovers, food, and the astronauts themselves!
  • ECLSS: Stands for Environmental Control and Life Support Systems. These are the machines that recycle astronaut sweat back into drinking water!

🚀 Career Pathway: ECLSS Systems Engineer

These engineers design the brilliant machines that scrub poisonous Carbon Dioxide from the air and keep the spaceship at a comfortable temperature. They literally keep humans alive in the deadly vacuum of space.

Middle School: Focus on Chemistry, Biology, and Earth Science.
College: Get a B.S. in Mechanical Engineering, Chemical Engineering, or Bioastronautics.
Grad School: Get a Master's Degree focusing on fluid dynamics or environmental control.
EYES ONLY

TEAM: SCIENCE (SCI)

Role: Planetary Astrobiologist

Your Mission: Pick the best place to land on Mars to search for alien life! You must balance the scientific reward (finding water) against the physical dangers of the landing site.

1. Landing Site Selection

Discuss with your team and circle one landing site:

Target Site Water Chance Hazards & Dangers
SITE A: Gale Crater Low Flat & Safe. (Boring science, but very safe).
SITE B: Polar Ice Cap High! Uneven Terrain! (Tell ENG to buy Landing Thrusters to prevent tipping over).
SITE C: Utopia Plains Medium Solar Storms! (Tell ENG to buy Radiation Shields).

2. Official NASA Math Calculation

Because Mars has less gravity and a thinner atmosphere than Earth, we land slower. If an Earth parachute lands at 300 mph, divide that by Mars's gravity factor (3) to find your Target Speed.

300 (Earth Speed) ÷ 3 (Gravity Factor) = Target Speed (mph)

📖 Space Glossary

  • Landing Thrusters: Small rocket engines on the bottom of a spaceship that fire right before touching the ground to slow the ship down and keep it balanced.
  • Solar Arrays: Giant "wings" made of glass and silicon that fold out of the spaceship to absorb sunlight and turn it into electricity to power the computers.

🚀 Career Pathway: Planetary Astrobiologist

Astrobiologists study extreme environments on Earth (like boiling hot springs or deep ocean vents) to figure out how alien life might survive on Mars or the icy moons of Jupiter!

Middle School: Join science fairs, study Biology, and look at the stars!
College: Get a B.S. in Biology, Geology, or Astronomy.
Grad School: Earn a Ph.D. in Planetary Science or Astrobiology, conducting real research in a lab!
