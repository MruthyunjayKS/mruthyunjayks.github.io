Educational Standards Alignment

Astrobiology Mission Control Simulation

The Astrobiology Mission Control game is a cross-curricular STEM simulation. It directly fulfills national standards (NGSS & CCSS) as well as the Arizona State Academic Standards in Science and Mathematics. The "Safe Mode" aligns with Middle School (Grades 6-8), while the "Adventure Mode" scales up to High School (Grades 9-12).

🔬 Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS)

Focus Area: Engineering Design & Space Systems

MS-ETS1-1 / HS-ETS1-1 (Engineering Design) "Define the criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a successful solution, taking into account relevant scientific principles and potential impacts..."
Game Application: The Engineering (ENG) team is given a strict constraint: a $100 Million budget. They must evaluate competing rocket engines and life support systems, balancing the constraints of cost against the criteria of safety, payload capacity, and distance required by the other teams.
MS-ETS1-2 / HS-ETS1-2 (Engineering Design) "Evaluate competing design solutions using a systematic process to determine how well they meet the criteria and constraints of the problem."
Game Application: The complex problem (getting to Mars safely) is broken down into distributed tasks (Navigation, Operations, Science). The teams must evaluate competing routes (Route A vs. Route B) and landing sites (Site A vs. B vs. C) by systematically weighing scientific rewards against hazards (radiation, asteroids, fuel depletion).

🌵 Arizona State Academic Standards

Focus Area: Arizona Mathematics Standards & Science and Engineering Practices (AzSS 2018)

AzSS SEP 5: Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking "Use mathematical representations to describe and/or support scientific conclusions and design solutions."
Game Application: Arizona's 2018 Science Standards mandate the use of SEPs. In this simulation, students cannot progress without using math to support their design. The NAV team must calculate Delta-V/Distance, and the SCI team calculates atmospheric refraction angles to design the solar array deployment.
AzSS SEP 6: Constructing Explanations and Designing Solutions "Design, evaluate, and/or refine a solution to a complex real-world problem, based on scientific knowledge, student-generated sources of evidence, prioritized criteria, and tradeoff considerations."
Game Application: The core of the game is tradeoff considerations. Students must refine their spacecraft design based on prioritized criteria (e.g., sacrificing a safe engine to afford radiation shielding to land at a site with a higher chance of finding water).
Arizona Math Standard: 6.RP.A.3 (Middle School) "Use ratio and rate reasoning to solve real-world and mathematical problems."
Game Application (Safe Mode): The Operations (OPS) team uses rate reasoning to calculate total food mass based on a rate of 500kg per astronaut. During anomalies, students calculate rate problems (e.g., losing 2 gallons of fuel per mile of course drift).
Arizona Math Standard: HS.N-Q.A.1 (High School) "Use units as a way to understand problems and to guide the solution of multi-step problems; choose and interpret units consistently in formulas."
Game Application (Adventure Mode): High school students calculate Delta-V (Δv) in kilometers per second (km/s) rather than miles. They must interpret the units of base velocity and gravitational slingshot boosts to find the exact injection velocity required to reach Mars.

📐 Common Core Mathematics Standards (CCSS-M)

Focus Area: Ratios, Proportional Relationships, and Quantities

CCSS.MATH.CONTENT.7.EE.B.3 (Middle School / Early High School) "Solve multi-step real-life and mathematical problems posed with positive and negative rational numbers in any form; apply properties of operations to calculate with numbers in any form."
Game Application (Safe & Adv Mode): The Operations team must calculate base mass, and then calculate and add a 10% safety buffer (multiplying by 1.10). The Science (SCI) team must offset Martian atmospheric scattering by adding precise degree values to geographic latitudes.

🤝 21st Century Skills & Crosscutting Concepts

Systems Thinking

Students experience how changes in one part of a system (e.g., the Navigator choosing a longer route) directly affect other parts of the system (the Engineer running out of budget for a reliable rocket engine).

Collaboration & Communication

The "Silence Protocol" forces students to practice precise, written technical communication. They learn that in STEM fields, sloppy handwriting or unclear instructions can result in catastrophic mission failure.
