NASA Career Profile: Astrodynamicist

A Day in the Life

As a Flight Dynamics Officer (FIDO) or Astrodynamicist, your job is to drive the spaceship. However, in space, there is no steering wheel. Everything is constantly moving. Planets orbit the sun, moons orbit planets, and gravity bends everything. You use advanced calculus and computer models to figure out exactly when to fire the engines, and for exactly how long, to thread a needle millions of miles away.

Expanded Glossary

  • Delta-V (Δv): "Change in Velocity." Space has no friction, so we don't measure "gallons of gas." We measure how much "push" (Delta-V) a rocket has to change its speed or direction.
  • Gravity Assist: Stealing momentum from a planet. By flying close to a planet, a spacecraft is pulled by its gravity, slingshotting it forward at incredible speeds.
  • Orbital Mechanics: The physics field dedicated entirely to how objects move under the influence of gravity in space.
  • Telemetry: The stream of data (speed, location, temperature) transmitted via radio waves from the spacecraft back to Earth.

🚀 The Flight Path: How to Get This Job

Middle School & High School

Focus heavily on Mathematics (Algebra, Calculus) and Physics. Join coding clubs or learn basic Python—astrodynamicists write a lot of code to run their math models!

Undergraduate Degree (College)

Earn a Bachelor of Science (B.S.) in Aerospace Engineering, Applied Mathematics, or Physics. Look for universities that participate in cube-sat (mini satellite) launches.

Graduate School & Internships

Many dynamicists hold Master's degrees. Apply for the NASA Pathways Internship program, which places college students directly into Mission Control for hands-on training.

The Career: Jet Propulsion Lab (JPL)

Work at NASA's JPL in California or Johnson Space Center in Texas. You could be the person calculating the descent path for the next Mars Rover!

TEAM: OPERATIONS (OPS)

Role: Life Support & Logistics

Your Mission: You must calculate the exact "Payload Mass" (weight) of the food, water, and oxygen. Space is unpredictable, so NASA requires a strict 10% Safety Buffer on all life-support math.

1. Mission Parameters

WAIT: Ask the NAV team which route they chose before starting!

  • Crew Size: 4 Astronauts
  • If NAV chose Route A (Fast): Base consumables = 330 kg per crew member.
  • If NAV chose Route B (Long): Base consumables = 550 kg per crew member.

2. Official NASA Math Calculation

Step 1: Multiply Crew Size by the Consumables per crew member.
Step 2: Multiply that total by 1.10 (to add the 10% safety buffer).

( 4 x kg ) * 1.10 = Total Mass

⚠️ TELL THE ENGINEER:

Give the Engineer your Total Mass. They must buy a Life Support system that can handle that weight, or the rocket will not launch.

⚠️ ALERT: MID-FLIGHT ANOMALIES

During transit, life support emergencies (like oxygen leaks or power failures) can occur. You will need to calculate leak rates or power divisions (division/subtraction) in seconds to keep the crew breathing. Stay sharp.

NASA Career Profile: ECLSS Engineer

A Day in the Life

An ECLSS (Environmental Control and Life Support Systems) Engineer keeps humans alive in environments trying to kill them. You design machines that scrub poisonous carbon dioxide out of the air, regulate extreme temperatures, and—most famously—turn astronaut sweat and urine back into perfectly clean drinking water. Every gram of water sent to space costs thousands of dollars, so nothing is wasted.

Expanded Glossary

  • Payload Mass: The total weight of the cargo, including food, scientific instruments, and the astronauts themselves.
  • Redundancy (Safety Buffer): Engineering systems so that if one thing breaks, a backup is already in place. In space, a single point of failure means death.
  • Scrubbers: Chemical filters (often using Lithium Hydroxide) that absorb CO2 from the air so astronauts don't suffocate on their own breath.
  • Closed-Loop System: A habitat where almost nothing leaves or enters. 98% of all water on the ISS is continuously recycled in a closed loop.

🚀 The Flight Path: How to Get This Job

Middle School & High School

Focus on Chemistry and Biology to understand how human bodies work and interact with gases. Join a FIRST Robotics team or a 3D printing club to learn how to build functional hardware.

Undergraduate Degree (College)

Earn a B.S. in Mechanical Engineering, Chemical Engineering, or Biomedical Engineering. You need to know how fluids and gases move through pipes and pumps.

Graduate School (Optional but helpful)

Specialize in Bioastronautics—the study of how to support human life in space. Universities like CU Boulder have dedicated labs for this.

The Career: Commercial Space & NASA

You might work for NASA, or private companies like SpaceX or Sierra Space, designing the air-flow systems for the next generation of space stations and lunar habitats.

TEAM: SCIENCE (SCI)

Role: Planetary Astrophysics

Your Mission: Pick a landing site on Mars. Then, you must calculate the exact angle to deploy the Solar Arrays based on the site's latitude and the Martian atmosphere. If the angle is wrong, the batteries will die during descent.

1. Landing Site Selection

Discuss with your team and circle one landing site:

Target Site Latitude Hazards & Dangers
SITE A: Gale Crater 5 Degrees Safe. (Flat terrain, low scientific yield).
SITE B: Polar Ice Cap 75 Degrees Ice Spikes! (Tell ENG to buy Landing Thrusters).
SITE C: Utopia Plains 35 Degrees Solar Storms! (Tell ENG to buy Radiation Shields).

2. Official NASA Math Calculation

To get maximum power, solar arrays must point at the sun. Add the Site Latitude to the Atmospheric Offset (+10 degrees) caused by Martian dust scattering.

(Latitude) + 10 (Offset) = Array Angle

⚠️ ALERT: MID-FLIGHT ANOMALIES

Space weather is violent. If a solar flare hits the ship, it may cause immediate cabin heating or radiation spikes. You will be relied upon to quickly calculate temperature changes to prevent the crew from overheating.

NASA Career Profile: Astrobiologist

A Day in the Life

Planetary scientists and astrobiologists are space detectives. You use data sent back from rovers and satellites to study the geology and chemistry of alien worlds. Your ultimate goal is finding the answer to humanity's biggest question: Are we alone? You might spend a month hiking in the freezing Antarctic or deep inside caves on Earth to understand how microscopic life survives, so you know exactly where to look on Mars.

Expanded Glossary

  • Atmospheric Scattering: Dust particles in the Martian atmosphere bend and absorb sunlight. Engineers must offset solar panels to catch the maximum amount of remaining light.
  • Extremophiles: Organisms on Earth (like tardigrades or certain bacteria) that thrive in extreme radiation, acid, or heat. They are the blueprint for alien life.
  • Biosignatures: Chemical footprints left behind by life (like certain methane gases or fossilized microbes in rock).
  • Latitude: The geographic coordinate measuring distance north or south of the equator, dictating sunlight angles.

🚀 The Flight Path: How to Get This Job

Middle School & High School

Enter science fairs. Take Earth Science, Biology, and Chemistry. Go outside and look at rocks, bugs, and the stars. Curiosity is your most important tool.

Undergraduate Degree (College)

Earn a B.S. in Geology, Biology, Chemistry, or Astronomy. Astrobiology is highly interdisciplinary, meaning you need to understand a little bit of everything.

Doctoral Degree (Ph.D.)

Most lead scientists at NASA hold a Ph.D. This involves spending 4-6 years after college doing original, hands-on research in a specific field, like planetary geology.

The Career: Principal Investigator

Work at universities, research institutes, or NASA Goddard. You could be the scientist writing the code that tells the Perseverance Rover which rock to drill into today.

NASA Career Profile: Systems / Flight Director

A Day in the Life

A rocket has millions of moving parts built by thousands of different people. A Systems Engineer ensures that the engine built in Alabama perfectly connects to the computer built in California. The Flight Director sits at the center of Mission Control. They don't need to know every equation, but they must understand how all the systems interact. When a crisis happens, the Flight Director listens to all the specialists and makes the final, high-pressure "Go/No-Go" decision.

Expanded Glossary

  • Max-Q: Maximum Dynamic Pressure. The exact moment during launch where aerodynamic stress (wind resistance) on the spaceship is the highest. If the rocket is too heavy or weak, it shatters here.
  • Systems Integration: The incredibly complex process of bringing together all the software, hardware, life support, and propulsion into one functioning vehicle.
  • Avionics: The electronic systems used on spacecraft, including navigation, communications, and multiple computers.
  • Go/No-Go: The famous roll-call where the Flight Director asks every single team (NAV, OPS, SCI) if their system is ready. One "No-Go" stops the launch.

🚀 The Flight Path: How to Get This Job

Middle School & High School

Focus on Math, Physics, and leadership. Join a robotics team, debate team, or student council. Learning how to manage people and communicate clearly is just as important as the math.

Undergraduate Degree (College)

Earn a B.S. in Systems Engineering, Aerospace Engineering, or Computer Science. Look for leadership roles in university engineering projects.

Early Career: Flight Controller

You don't start as the Director. You start as a Flight Controller at a specific console (like OPS or NAV), proving you can handle intense pressure and make correct calls during simulations.

The Peak: Flight Director

After years of flawless performance, you are selected to lead the room. Flight Directors at NASA get to choose their own "Call Sign" color (e.g., "Flight is Go. Kranz is White Flight").
