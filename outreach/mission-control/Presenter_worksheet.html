Facilitation Guide

Crafting a Meaningful Science Activity

Introduction Worksheet: Astrobiology Mission Control

(Estimated Time: 5–8 Minutes)

1. Start with Curiosity (The Hook)

What is one big question at the heart of your lesson?

"How do you keep a crew of 4 alive on a 140-million-mile road trip to Mars when everything goes wrong?"

Now simplify it so it’s accessible to middle/high school youth:

"How do you fly a spaceship to Mars if you can't use a computer to talk to each other?"

2. Connect to Lived Experience (People)

What everyday experience connects to your topic?

Trying to plan a group trip with friends on a strict budget, OR playing a co-op video game where one person's mistake hurts the whole team.

Opening prompt I will ask students:

"Have you ever worked on a group project where one person didn't do their part, and it messed up the whole thing? Imagine that, but instead of a bad grade, you run out of oxygen. How do you make sure your team doesn't fail?"

3. Establish Relevance (Why This Matters)

Why should a 10–18-year-old care about this topic?

It's about survival, communication, and extreme high-stakes teamwork. It shows them that space isn't just about knowing facts; it's about solving puzzles under pressure.

Now turn that into one sentence you can say aloud:

"Because exploring space isn't just about building the biggest rockets—it's about teamwork, communication, and making life-or-death decisions when you have limited resources."

4. Introduce the Big Idea (Place + Practice)

In one clear sentence: "Today we are going to explore how ________."

"Today we are going to explore how NASA Mission Control teams use math, engineering, and budgeting to safely guide astronauts to Mars."

What tools or methods will students engage with?

"You will use a hard $100 Million budget, exact payload math, and a silent 'Paper LAN' communication system to launch a live rocket simulation."

5. Humanize the Science (Personnel)

What story or human element will I include?

"When the Apollo 13 oxygen tank exploded in space, it wasn't the astronauts who figured out how to get home. It was thousands of engineers on the ground in Mission Control, locked in a room, using pencils, paper, and math to save their friends' lives."

6. Introduce Wonder or Uncertainty

Include one statement like: "We don't know the answer yet."

"Space is incredibly unpredictable. Even if you do all the math perfectly today and buy the best rocket, we don't know what solar flares, asteroid fields, or anomalies the universe is going to throw at you during the flight."

7. End the Introduction with an Invitation

Complete this sentence: "By the end of today, you will be able to..." OR "Today, you're going to think like a ______."

"Today, you are going to think like a NASA Flight Director. You're not the astronauts; you're the geniuses on the ground keeping them alive."

Quick Self-Check (5Ps Integration)

  • Did I connect to students’ lived experience? (People - Group projects/budgeting)
  • Did I reference environment or context? (Place - The bunker/Mission Control)
  • Did I mention how science is practiced? (Practice - Math, limits, paper comms)
  • Did I humanize scientists or teams? (Personnel - Apollo 13 ground crew)
  • Did I hint at future pathways or roles? (Profession - Flight Directors)

My identity-building sentence (End with something that builds belonging):

"You don't have to be an astronaut to explore space. Science is for the people who stay curious and work together to solve the impossible."
